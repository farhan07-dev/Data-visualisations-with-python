Final analysis¶

Singapore’s government healthcare expenditure has a upward trend over the years, with three major spikes which happens between 2007–2009, 2012–2014, and 2018–2020. These increases in expenditure correlates with both chronic and infectious disease. The period from 2012 to 2014, aligns with the rise in chronic disease prevalence mainly obesity and diabetes suggesting that healthcare funds were likely directed toward the management and prevention of long-term health conditions. Infectious diseases such as Dengue Fever have periodic surges, mainly in 2014 and 2018 to 2020. These years correspond with the spikes in healthcare expenditure, indicating government spending in response to outbreak severity. Meanwhile, Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD), despite having the highest number of reported cases, did not show a direct correlation with expenditure increases, implying it may be treated as a recurring cost within standard health budgets. Variability in dengue outbreaks, particularly in 2014, was matched by increased spending and was followed by reduced outbreak severity in subsequent years—suggesting that prior investments may have enhanced public health resilience. The most dramatic rise in expenditure occurred in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic most likely due to urgent spending on containment, testing, and healthcare infrastructure. These patterns suggest that Singapore’s government has generally aligned healthcare spending with health threats, demonstrating a balance between proactive and reactive allocation of resources. Hence we can conclude that the budget for healthcare is being allocated effectively.
